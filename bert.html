What BERT is

BERT is a transformer-encoder-only model. Unlike GPT (decoder-only, autoregressive), every token attends to every other token in both directions at every layer. That bidirectionality is the whole point.

  • Released: October 2018 by Devlin et al. (Google AI Language)
  • Architecture: stack of transformer encoder blocks (multi-head self-attention + feed-forward)
  • Pre-training: self-supervised on BooksCorpus (800M words) + English Wikipedia (2.5B words)
  • Fine-tuning: add a small task head, train end-to-end on labeled data
The key insight: a model that has read billions of sentences and learned to fill in blanks knows a lot about language. Plug a tiny classifier on top and it punches far above its weight on downstream tasks.
02 /

Pre-training objectives

Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

Randomly mask 15% of WordPiece tokens. The model predicts the originals. Loss: cross-entropy over the masked positions only.

Of those 15%:

  • 80% replaced with [MASK]
  • 10% replaced with a random token
  • 10% kept unchanged

The 10/10 trick prevents the model from being lazy and only producing strong outputs at [MASK] positions during fine-tuning, where there are no masks.

Why bidirectional? A unidirectional language model can't condition on future tokens without leakage. MLM lets every position see the full bidirectional context.

Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

Given two sentences A and B, predict whether B actually follows A in the corpus (50%) or is a random sentence from elsewhere (50%). Binary classification on the [CLS] token's final hidden state.

Input:  [CLS] A₁ A₂ ... [SEP] B₁ B₂ ... [SEP]
Label:  IsNext / NotNext
Hot take: RoBERTa (2019) showed NSP is mostly useless and dropped it. SBERT replaced it with sentence-pair contrastive objectives. Modern BERT-style models often skip NSP entirely.

Combined loss

BERT's pre-training loss is the sum of MLM and NSP losses:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{NSP}}$$

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} = -\sum_{i \in M} \log P(x_i \mid \tilde{x})$$

where $M$ is the set of masked positions and $\tilde{x}$ is the corrupted input.

Input format

Every input position's vector is the sum of three embeddings: token + segment + position. All three are learned tables of the same hidden size $d$ (e.g. 768).

$$\mathbf{x}_i = \mathbf{E}_{\text{tok}}(t_i) + \mathbf{E}_{\text{seg}}(s_i) + \mathbf{E}_{\text{pos}}(i)$$

What's inside one encoder block

Each of the L blocks is the standard "post-LN" transformer encoder:

  1. Multi-head self-attention with H/A dimensions per head
  2. Residual + LayerNorm
  3. Position-wise feed-forward: linear → GELU → linear (hidden size 4H)
  4. Residual + LayerNorm

$$\text{Attn}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\tfrac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

Self-attention is full and bidirectional — no causal mask.

WordPiece tokenization

BERT uses a 30,522-token WordPiece vocabulary. Unknown words are split into known sub-pieces, with continuation pieces prefixed by ##.

"playing"     → ["play", "##ing"]
"unaffable"   → ["un", "##aff", "##able"]
"transformer" → ["transform", "##er"]
"BERT"        → ["bert"]   (lowercased in bert-base-uncased)

Fine-tuning paradigm

One pre-trained BERT, three (and many more) downstream task patterns. In each case you add a small task-specific head and train end-to-end with a low learning rate (typically 2e-5 to 5e-5) for 2-4 epochs.

Sequence classification

Sentiment, NLI, topic. Use the final [CLS] hidden state.

[CLS] → Linear(H→C) → softmax

Examples: SST-2, MNLI, QNLI

Token classification

NER, POS tagging. Apply the head to every token's hidden state.

h_i → Linear(H→C) → softmax (per token)

Examples: CoNLL-2003 NER

Span extraction (QA)

Predict start & end positions of the answer span.

h_i → 2 logits (start, end)

Examples: SQuAD 1.1 / 2.0

Bidirectional vs autoregressive

BERT (encoder-only)

Predicting cute in "my dog is __" with full context:

my dog is [MASK] today .

Attends to both sides. Cannot generate text autoregressively, but produces strong contextual representations.

GPT (decoder-only)

Predicting the next token after "my dog is":

my dog is ? today .

Causal mask blocks future tokens. Natural fit for generation, weaker for understanding tasks at the same parameter count (historically — the gap has narrowed).

Limitations and successors

BERT's weaknesses

  • Quadratic attention in sequence length → max 512 tokens
  • No generation — encoder-only, you can't sample
  • Pre-training/fine-tuning mismatch — [MASK] never appears at inference
  • NSP is weak signal — later models drop it
  • Sentence embeddings are bad out of the box; mean-pooled [CLS] underperforms

Successors and variants

ModelYearTwist
RoBERTa2019Drop NSP, more data, longer training, dynamic masking
ALBERT2019Parameter sharing across layers; sentence-order prediction instead of NSP
DistilBERT2019Knowledge distillation, ~40% smaller, ~60% faster, retains ~97%

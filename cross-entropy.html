Cross-Entropy, Demystified

The loss function quietly powering every transformer language model. Drag sliders, type formulas, fight a boss, and walk away knowing why F.cross_entropy is what it is.

1 Information Theory Roots

Where does cross-entropy come from? Three quantities, one story.

Entropy — surprise, on average

Shannon's entropy of a distribution $p$ measures the expected number of bits (or nats, depending on the log base) needed to describe an outcome:

$$ H(p) = -\sum_{x} p(x) \log p(x) $$

Coin flip with $p=0.5$: maximally uncertain, $H = 1$ bit. A loaded coin with $p=0.99$: nearly no surprise, $H \approx 0.08$ bit.

KL divergence — the "extra cost" of being wrong

If reality is $p$ but you encode using model $q$, you pay extra. That extra is the Kullback-Leibler divergence:

$$ \mathrm{KL}(p \,\|\, q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \ \geq\ 0 $$

Cross-entropy = entropy + KL

$$ H(p, q) = -\sum_x p(x) \log q(x) = H(p) + \mathrm{KL}(p \,\|\, q) $$

Since $H(p)$ is fixed by the data, minimizing cross-entropy with respect to $q$ is exactly the same as minimizing $\mathrm{KL}(p \,\|\, q)$. That is why we can train models on $H(p,q)$ and still claim we are matching the data distribution.

Take-away: cross-entropy is the price a model pays for its imperfect beliefs, in the units the data demands.

2 The Formula

$$ H(p, q) = -\sum_{x} p(x) \log q(x) $$

In supervised classification the target $p$ is usually one-hot: $p(y) = 1$ for the true class $y$, $0$ everywhere else. The sum collapses:

$$ H(p, q) = -\log q(y) $$

This is the negative log-likelihood (NLL) of the true class. If your model says "the right answer has probability 0.9", loss is $-\log 0.9 \approx 0.105$. If it says 0.01, loss is $\approx 4.6$. Confidence is rewarded; ignorance is punished — gently if you're not too sure, harshly if you're confidently wrong.

4 Why a Logarithm?

Two reasons.

Likelihood multiplies; loss should add. Across $N$ independent examples the joint likelihood is $\prod_i q_i(y_i)$. Taking $-\log$ turns a product into a sum, so total loss is the per-example losses added up — exactly what we want for SGD.

$$ -\log \prod_i q_i(y_i) = \sum_i -\log q_i(y_i) $$

Maximum likelihood, dressed differently. Minimizing average $-\log q(y)$ is the same as maximizing $\log$-likelihood, which is the same as maximizing likelihood. So cross-entropy training is just MLE in a tuxedo.

Bonus: $-\log$ is convex, blows up only at $q = 0$, and saturates gently as $q \to 1$. The gradient stays meaningful exactly where you want it: when the model is wrong.

5 Softmax + NLL = Cross-Entropy

Models output unnormalized scores ("logits") $z \in \mathbb{R}^K$. We turn them into a distribution with softmax:

$$ q_i = \mathrm{softmax}(z)_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} $$

Then plug into NLL: $L = -\log q_y$. Naively this is two steps and numerically dangerous (the $e^{z_i}$ can overflow). The trick is log-softmax with the max-shift:

$$ \log q_i = z_i - \max_k z_k - \log \sum_j e^{z_j - \max_k z_k} $$

So F.cross_entropy(logits, target) in PyTorch and tf.nn.sparse_softmax_cross_entropy_with_logits in TF accept logits directly — they fuse log-softmax and NLL for stability and speed. Calling softmax yourself first and then NLL is a common bug that quietly worsens training.

# PyTorch
loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, target)   # logits, not probs!

# Manual (don't ship this, it's just for clarity)
log_probs = F.log_softmax(logits, dim=-1)
loss = -log_probs.gather(-1, target.unsqueeze(-1)).mean()

6 Per-Token CE in Language Modeling

7 The Gradient: Beautifully Simple

The reason softmax + cross-entropy is so popular is the gradient. Let $z$ be the logits, $q = \mathrm{softmax}(z)$, and $y$ the one-hot target. Then:

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = q_i - y_i $$

Derivation

$L = -\sum_k y_k \log q_k$. Using $\partial q_k / \partial z_i = q_k(\delta_{ki} - q_i)$:

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = -\sum_k y_k \frac{1}{q_k} \cdot q_k(\delta_{ki} - q_i) = -\sum_k y_k(\delta_{ki} - q_i) = q_i \sum_k y_k - y_i = q_i - y_i $$

The gradient is just prediction minus target. Sparse, well-scaled, and the same form whether the target is one-hot or a soft distribution.

8 Label Smoothing

Instead of betting everything on one class, leave a sliver of probability mass on the others.

For smoothing factor $\varepsilon$ and $K$ classes:

$$ \tilde p_k = (1 - \varepsilon)\, y_k + \frac{\varepsilon}{K} $$

This prevents the model from becoming overconfident, improves calibration, and tends to help generalization. The loss is computed against the smoothed target, not one-hot.

9 Cross-Entropy ↔ Perplexity

Language model people love to quote perplexity. It is just cross-entropy in pretty clothes:

$$ \mathrm{PPL} = \exp\bigl( \overline{H}(p, q) \bigr) $$

Where $\overline{H}$ is the mean per-token cross-entropy. A perplexity of 9.97 means the model is "as confused as if it were uniformly choosing among ~10 words at every step". A 1.0 perplexity is impossible-good; a vocab-size perplexity is random-uniform.

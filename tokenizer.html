Chapter 01 · The tongue of every transformer

String in,
integers out.

Models do not read characters. They do not read words. They read integers. A tokenizer is the small program that translates raw text into the integer language a transformer actually consumes — and translates the integers it generates back into text you can read. Without it, GPT, BERT, LLaMA, Claude — all of them are silent.

in one sentence
A tokenizer is a function $T : \text{string} \to \mathbb{Z}^n$ that breaks text into tokens from a fixed vocabulary and emits their integer IDs. A detokenizer $T^{-1}$ does the reverse. 
"hello world"
       │
       ▼  encode
[ 31373, 995 ]      # two tokens, GPT-2 vocab
       │
       ▼  decode
"hello world"
why not just use words?
  • Vocab explosion. English has 1M+ word forms; a model would need a 1M-row embedding table.
  • Out-of-vocab. A typo or new word becomes <UNK> and the model is blind.
  • Morphology. "running" / "runner" / "runs" share a root the model never sees.
  • Languages. Chinese has no spaces. Turkish glues morphemes. Whitespace splitting fails.
Subword tokenizers fix all four — a small vocab of ~30k–200k pieces covers any string by composition.
three families you'll meet

BPE Byte-Pair Encoding — merges the most frequent adjacent pair, repeatedly. GPT-2/3/4, LLaMA, Mistral.

WordPiece Like BPE but picks the merge that maximizes corpus likelihood. BERT, DistilBERT, ELECTRA.

SentencePiece Treats whitespace as a regular character (▁); language-agnostic. Wraps either Unigram or BPE. T5, ALBERT, LLaMA.

what comes next
Tokens become token IDs become embeddings become a tensor of shape [batch, seq_len, d_model]. Every other piece of a transformer — attention, FFN, output projection — is just transformations of that tensor. Get tokenization right, the rest is matrix multiplication.

Chapter 02 · Five steps every tokenizer takes

How a tokenizer
actually works.

Every modern tokenizer ends up doing the same five things in some order: normalize, pre-tokenize, split into subwords using a learned merge table, map each piece to an ID, and add special tokens. Here is each step, in plain English.

1

Normalize

Lowercase / NFC-unicode / strip accents — depending on the model. GPT-2 does almost nothing here. BERT-uncased does aggressive lowercasing.

"Hèllo!" → "hello!" (BERT-uncased)
2

Pre-tokenize

Split on whitespace and punctuation so the merge step never crosses word boundaries. GPT-2 uses a regex; SentencePiece does not pre-split — it operates on raw bytes including spaces.

"hello world!" → ["hello", " world", "!"]
3

Apply the merge / vocab table

The slow training-time step learns a vocabulary of subword pieces. At inference, each pre-token is split into the longest known pieces.

"tokenization" → ["token", "ization"] (BPE)
4

Map pieces → integer IDs

The vocabulary is a dictionary str → int. Each piece becomes its row index in the embedding table.

["token", "ization"] → [30001, 1634]
5

Add special tokens & pad

BERT prepends [CLS] and ends with [SEP]. GPT-style models often add nothing. Batched inputs are padded to a common length with a <pad> id and an attention mask records which positions are real.

[101, 30001, 1634, 102, 0, 0] mask: [1, 1, 1, 1, 0, 0]

02 BPE in 60 seconds — the merging step

Byte-Pair Encoding builds its vocabulary during training by repeatedly finding the most frequent adjacent symbol pair in the corpus and adding their concatenation as a new symbol. Run for $V$ merges, you get a vocab of size ~initial bytes + $V$.

a tiny worked example
Start with the corpus "low low low lower newer" and split into characters with a word-boundary marker ▁: 
# step 0 — bytes
▁ l o w   ▁ l o w   ▁ l o w   ▁ l o w e r   ▁ n e w e r

# most frequent pair = (l, o)  →  merge to "lo"
▁ lo w   ▁ lo w   ▁ lo w   ▁ lo w e r   ▁ n e w e r

# most frequent pair = (lo, w)  →  merge to "low"
▁ low   ▁ low   ▁ low   ▁ low e r   ▁ n e w e r

# most frequent pair = (e, r)  →  merge to "er"
▁ low   ▁ low   ▁ low   ▁ low er   ▁ n e w er

# final vocab includes: ▁, l, o, w, e, r, n, lo, low, er, ...
Inference walks the same merge list in order and applies any merge that still fits. That's why BPE is deterministic and lossless — every byte sequence has a unique tokenization.

03 Reading a tokenization in your head

A few tells once you've stared at GPT-2 output for a while:

  • A leading space matters. "hello" at the start of a string is a different token from " hello" mid-sentence. GPT-2 even prints leading spaces in the byte-level vocab as Ġ.
  • Common words → 1 token. "the", "and", "is" are single tokens.
  • Long / rare words → many tokens. "tokenization" splits, "antidisestablishmentarianism" really splits.
  • Numbers and emoji get chopped fine — "2024" may be 1, 2, or 4 tokens depending on the tokenizer.
  • Non-Latin scripts in byte-level BPE become byte tokens: a 3-byte UTF-8 codepoint costs 3 tokens. SentencePiece avoids this when trained on the language.

Chapter 04 · Where the tensors come from

From string to logits
in eight shapes.

Picking the same sentence — "Hello, world!" — let's track the exact shapes from string to logits. B = batch, T = sequence length, D = hidden size, V = vocab size.

1

raw string

"Hello, world!"

shape: scalar (no tensor yet)
2

tokenize → list of ids

[15496, 11, 995, 0] (GPT-2 BPE, 4 tokens)

shape: [T=4] dtype: int64
3

batch + pad

Stack two sentences in a batch, pad to T=6 with id 0:
[[15496, 11, 995, 0, 0, 0],
 [15496, 11, 5440, 16085, 0, 0]]

input_ids: [B=2, T=6] attention_mask: [B=2, T=6]
4

embedding lookup

x = embed[input_ids] — one row of a [V × D] table per id.

x: [B=2, T=6, D=768] embed: [V=50257, D=768]
5

+ positional embedding

Add a [T × D] table (or RoPE rotates Q/K instead). Now positions matter.

x: [B=2, T=6, D=768] pos: [T=6, D=768]
6

L transformer blocks

Each block keeps the shape. Inside, attention briefly produces scores: [B, H, T, T].

x stays: [B=2, T=6, D=768]
7

output head → logits

logits = x @ embed.T — one score per vocab token at every position.

logits: [B=2, T=6, V=50257]
8

softmax → next-token id

argmax(logits[:, -1, :], dim=-1) picks the next id; the tokenizer turns it back into text.

next_id: [B=2] "decoded text" via T⁻¹

04 The same flow, in PyTorch

end-to-end with HuggingFace
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForCausalLM
import torch

tok   = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("gpt2")
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("gpt2")

# 1. encode → dict of int64 tensors
batch = tok(["Hello, world!", "Hello, friends, again"],
            padding=True, return_tensors="pt")
print(batch.input_ids.shape)        # torch.Size([2, 6])
print(batch.attention_mask.shape)   # torch.Size([2, 6])

# 2. forward — embedding + transformer blocks
out = model(**batch)
print(out.logits.shape)            # torch.Size([2, 6, 50257])

# 3. greedy next-token = argmax over vocab axis
next_id = out.logits[:, -1, :].argmax(dim=-1)
print(tok.batch_decode(next_id.unsqueeze(-1)))
Memory back-of-envelope. An 8k-context, batch-of-1 inference at fp16 needs roughly $T \times D \times 2$ bytes per layer for the activation, plus a KV cache of $2 \times L \times T \times D \times 2$ bytes. For LLaMA-7B that's about ~512 MB of KV cache at T=8192 — a budget your tokenizer's compression ratio directly controls.
Chapter 05 · Three families of subword

BPE · WordPiece · SentencePiece.

All three produce subword tokens, all three start from frequency statistics, but the merge rule and the way they handle whitespace differ. Type a string and see how each splits it.

BPE — frequency merges

Pick the most frequent adjacent pair, merge, repeat. Inference applies the same merges in order. Greedy and lossless.

Used by: GPT-2/3/4, RoBERTa, LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen.

Tell: leading spaces are part of the token (printed as Ġ or ▁).

WordPiece — likelihood merges

Like BPE, but the merge rule maximizes corpus log-likelihood instead of raw frequency. Continuation pieces are prefixed ##.

Used by: BERT, DistilBERT, ELECTRA, MobileBERT.

Tell: "playing" → ["play", "##ing"]

SentencePiece — bytes are sacred

Treats whitespace as a real character (▁ = U+2581) and learns directly on raw text — no pre-tokenization. Wraps either Unigram-LM or BPE.

Used by: T5, ALBERT, XLNet, mT5, LLaMA.

Tell: tokens like ▁hello, ▁world.

05 What changes if you pick the wrong one?

  • Tokenizer mismatch is silent and catastrophic. Train with BPE, infer with WordPiece — every embedding lookup is wrong. The model still runs and the output is gibberish.
  • Adding a token at runtime is allowed (e.g. for chat templates), but you must resize the embedding table to match. Forget that step and you crash on the new id.
  • Different languages, different bills. Korean in GPT-2 BPE costs ~3× the tokens vs English. Korean in a Korean-trained SentencePiece costs ~1×. Same model, different cost.

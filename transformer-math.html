The path from numbers to attention

A walk through every piece of math you actually need to read the original Transformer paper without flinching. Numbers are real, derivations are short, and you can poke at every formula.

1 · Linear algebra

Vectors, matrices, dot product

A vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$ is an ordered list of $d$ real numbers, e.g. $\mathbf{x} = (x_1,\dots,x_d)$. We treat it as a column unless stated otherwise.

The dot product of $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is

$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^{d} u_i v_i = \|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta.$$

So dot product = magnitude × magnitude × cosine of the angle. When both are unit-length, dot product = cosine.

A matrix $A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ has $m$ rows and $n$ columns. Multiplication of $A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ by $B\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ gives $AB\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times p}$ with

$$(AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} A_{ik}\,B_{kj}.$$

The shape rule: $(m,\!\underline{n})\cdot(\underline{n},\!p)\to(m,p)$. The inner dims must match, the outer dims survive. Memorize this and 80% of transformer-shape headaches go away.

2 · Embeddings

Tokens become vectors

An embedding turns a discrete token id $t\in\{0,\dots,V-1\}$ into a vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$ via a lookup table $E\in\mathbb{R}^{V\times d}$:

$$\text{emb}(t) = E_{t,:} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}.$$

For a sequence of $n$ tokens we get $X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$. Tokens with similar meanings end up nearby in this space — that's why dot products will become useful as similarity.

Sketch (d=2) — semantically similar tokens form small angles → high dot product → high attention later on.

3 · Similarity

Why dot products are similarity

Given $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^d$, define cosine similarity

$$\cos(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|} \in [-1, 1].$$

Two unit vectors pointing the same way give 1; orthogonal give 0; opposite give -1. Inside attention we use the raw dot product (no normalization) — large magnitudes do shout louder, which is partly the point and partly why we'll later need to rescale.

Quick proof of the cosine identity

By the law of cosines, $\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 - 2\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$. Expanding the LHS gives $\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 - 2\,\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$. Equate and cancel: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta.$

4 · Softmax

Softmax — turning scores into probabilities

Given scores $\mathbf{z}=(z_1,\dots,z_n)\in\mathbb{R}^n$, the softmax is

$$\sigma(\mathbf{z})_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} e^{z_j}}.$$

Properties:

  • $\sigma_i\in(0,1)$ and $\sum_i\sigma_i=1$ — it's a probability distribution.
  • Translation invariant: $\sigma(\mathbf{z}+c\mathbf{1})=\sigma(\mathbf{z})$ — so we can subtract the max for numerical stability.
  • Smooth, differentiable, with $\partial\sigma_i/\partial z_j = \sigma_i(\delta_{ij}-\sigma_j)$.

Temperature. The temperature-scaled softmax is

$$\sigma(\mathbf{z}/T)_i = \frac{e^{z_i/T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j/T}}.$$

Small $T$ (cold) → spiky, deterministic-ish distribution. Large $T$ (hot) → flat, near-uniform. Move the slider:

scores z = [2.0, 1.0, 0.5, -1.0, 0.2] · T = 1.00

Notice: as $T\to 0$ the bar over the largest score grows to ~1 (argmax). As $T\to\infty$ all bars approach $1/n$.

5 · Attention

Scaled dot-product attention, with real numbers

Pack queries, keys, values into matrices $Q\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_k}$, $K\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_k}$, $V\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_v}$. Then

$$\text{Attn}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V.$$

Step by step on a 2-token toy example with $d_k=d_v=2$:

$$Q=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix},\quad K=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix},\quad V=\begin{pmatrix}10 & 0\\ 0 & 10\end{pmatrix}.$$

① Scores $S = QK^\top$:

$$S = \begin{pmatrix}1\!\cdot\!1+0\!\cdot\!1 & 1\!\cdot\!0+0\!\cdot\!1\\ 0\!\cdot\!1+1\!\cdot\!1 & 0\!\cdot\!0+1\!\cdot\!1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 1 & 1\end{pmatrix}.$$

② Scale by $\sqrt{d_k}=\sqrt{2}\approx1.414$:

$$S/\sqrt{2} \approx \begin{pmatrix}0.707 & 0\\ 0.707 & 0.707\end{pmatrix}.$$

③ Row-wise softmax:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}\sigma(0.707,0)\\ \sigma(0.707,0.707)\end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix}0.670 & 0.330\\ 0.500 & 0.500\end{pmatrix}.$$

④ Output $AV$:

$$AV \approx \begin{pmatrix}0.670\!\cdot\!10+0.330\!\cdot\!0 & 0.670\!\cdot\!0+0.330\!\cdot\!10\\ 5 & 5\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}6.70 & 3.30\\ 5.00 & 5.00\end{pmatrix}.$$

Token 1 mostly attended to itself (its key matched best), so its output is closer to $V_1=(10,0)$. Token 2's query was equally close to both keys, so its output is the centroid $(5,5)$. Attention = soft, weighted, learned lookup.

6 · Why √d_k?

The variance argument

Suppose entries of $Q$ and $K$ are independent, each with mean 0 and variance 1. A single score is

$$s = \mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{k} = \sum_{i=1}^{d_k} q_i k_i.$$

Then $\mathbb{E}[s]=0$ and, by independence,

$$\text{Var}(s) = \sum_{i=1}^{d_k} \text{Var}(q_i k_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{d_k} \mathbb{E}[q_i^2]\,\mathbb{E}[k_i^2] = d_k.$$

So the standard deviation is $\sqrt{d_k}$. As $d_k$ grows, scores blow up and softmax saturates — gradients vanish. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ rescales variance back to 1 and keeps softmax in its sweet spot.

Why softmax saturation kills learning

If one score is much larger than the rest, $\sigma_i\!\approx\!1$ for that index and $\!\approx\!0$ elsewhere. The Jacobian $\partial\sigma_i/\partial z_j=\sigma_i(\delta_{ij}-\sigma_j)$ contains products like $\sigma_i\sigma_j\!\approx\!0$, so gradients flowing back through that softmax are nearly zero — no signal to update $W_Q$ and $W_K$.

7 · Multi-head

Reshape, attend, concat

With $h$ heads and model dim $d_{\text{model}}$, we set $d_k = d_v = d_{\text{model}}/h$. Each head $i$ has its own projections:

$$Q_i = X W_i^Q,\ K_i = X W_i^K,\ V_i = X W_i^V,\quad W_i^Q\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}\times d_k}.$$

Compute $\text{head}_i=\text{Attn}(Q_i,K_i,V_i)\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_v}$, then

$$\text{MHA}(X) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1,\dots,\text{head}_h)\,W^O,\quad W^O\in\mathbb{R}^{(h d_v)\times d_{\text{model}}}.$$

In code, instead of $h$ separate matmuls, we do one and reshape:

  • $X\!\to\!XW^Q\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_{\text{model}}}$
  • reshape to $(n, h, d_k)$, transpose to $(h, n, d_k)$
  • do batched attention → $(h, n, d_v)$
  • transpose+reshape back to $(n, h\cdot d_v)$, project with $W^O$

Multi-head ≠ "more parameters". With $d_k = d_{\text{model}}/h$, the parameter count of MHA matches a single-head with $d_k=d_{\text{model}}$. Multi-head buys you multiple subspaces of attention with the same compute.

8 · LayerNorm

Layer normalization

For an input vector $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$, with learned $\gamma,\beta\in\mathbb{R}^d$ and small $\varepsilon>0$:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i,\quad \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d}(x_i-\mu)^2,$$
$$\text{LN}(\mathbf{x})_i = \gamma_i\,\frac{x_i - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \varepsilon}} + \beta_i.$$

Normalizes across features per token (BatchNorm normalizes across the batch, which transformers don't like because sequence lengths vary). The learnable $\gamma,\beta$ let the network undo normalization if it wants.

9 · Cross-entropy

Cross-entropy for next-token prediction

Suppose the model outputs logits $\mathbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^V$ for vocab size $V$. The predicted distribution is $\hat{p} = \sigma(\mathbf{z})$. For a true next-token $y\in\{1,\dots,V\}$ (one-hot $\mathbf{y}$):

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{c=1}^{V} y_c\log\hat{p}_c = -\log\hat{p}_y = -z_y + \log\sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{z_j}.$$

Notice the right-hand form — that's the famous logsumexp trick — used directly because it's numerically stable and its derivative is clean:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_i} = \hat{p}_i - y_i.$$

"Predicted minus target" — that's the gradient at the output of softmax + cross-entropy. Beautifully simple, and it is why classifiers train so cleanly.

10 · Gradients

Gradient intuition for the attention block

Let $A=\sigma(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_k})$ and $O=AV$. With upstream gradient $\mathrm{d}O$ of the same shape as $O$:

  • $\mathrm{d}V = A^\top \mathrm{d}O$ — values get gradient weighted by how much they were attended to (rows that received high attention get bigger updates).
  • $\mathrm{d}A = \mathrm{d}O\,V^\top$ — gradient on attention weights.
  • Through softmax (per row): $\mathrm{d}S_{ij} = A_{ij}\big(\mathrm{d}A_{ij} - \sum_k A_{ik}\mathrm{d}A_{ik}\big)$.
  • Then $\mathrm{d}Q = \mathrm{d}S\,K/\sqrt{d_k}$ and $\mathrm{d}K = \mathrm{d}S^\top Q/\sqrt{d_k}$.

Three intuitions worth keeping:

  1. Tokens that were attended to are the ones whose value-vector parameters get a strong update — attention routes credit.
  2. The softmax derivative subtracts the row-mean of the attention-weighted gradient; this is what enforces the "stay on the simplex" constraint.
  3. If $S$ is saturated (one entry near 1), $A_{ij}(1-A_{ij})\!\approx\!0$ — gradient dies. Hence again: scale by $\sqrt{d_k}$.

That's the whole math stack: linear algebra → embeddings → similarity → softmax → attention → scaling → multi-head → norm → loss → gradients. Now go play with it.
